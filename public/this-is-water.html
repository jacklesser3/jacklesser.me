Mindware — A Timeless Reflection

This Is Water

David Foster Wallace — Kenyon College, 2005
~22 min listen 5 reflections One commencement speech
Recording soon

Two young fish are swimming along when they pass an older fish going the other way. He nods and says, “Morning, boys. How’s the water?”

The two young fish swim on for a while. Eventually one of them looks at the other and asks, “What the hell is water?”

The parable that opens the speech
I

The Two Fish

The speech is about the parable. The parable is about adult life. The point Wallace is gentle about — almost embarrassed to deliver — is that the most important realities are the hardest to see. They’re the water you’re already swimming in.

Most of what matters isn’t hidden behind some advanced practice or special knowledge. It’s right here, surrounding you, so constant and obvious that your brain has stopped registering it. The work isn’t to find new water. The work is to notice the water you’ve been in this whole time.

The most obvious, important realities are often the ones that are hardest to see and talk about. David Foster Wallace
II

Main Character Syndrome

You walk through life as the main character of your own movie. Everyone else is a side character — the guy in front of you in line, the woman on her phone in traffic, the cashier who won’t make eye contact. It’s not a choice you made. It’s the operating system.

Wallace is generous about this. He doesn’t call it a moral failing. He calls it the default — biology’s gift and burden. Every experience you’ve ever had has been routed through your own head. Of course you feel central. The miracle is that anyone, ever, manages to feel otherwise.

Awareness is what lets you remember that every one of those side characters is the main character of their own movie — with the same hunger, the same fear, the same long bad day you’re having. That remembering isn’t automatic. It has to be chosen, and re-chosen, every time the line at the grocery store starts to slow down.

III

The Real Value of a Real Education

Wallace makes a quiet argument that “learning how to think” doesn’t mean stockpiling knowledge. It means having some control over what and how you pay attention. That’s it. That’s the whole point of education, in his telling.

The freedom isn’t the freedom to win arguments or earn more or know more things. It’s the freedom to construct meaning out of an experience instead of having the meaning constructed for you by your default settings. It’s the daily, small, unspectacular act of choosing the story.

Learning how to think really means learning how to exercise some control over how and what you think. David Foster Wallace
IV

The Supermarket and the Traffic Jam

The whole moral argument lives in the mundane. Wallace doesn’t reach for the dramatic example. He reaches for the boring grocery store after a long day. The slow-moving SUV in front of you. The rude person at the register. The fluorescent lights.

The default story is that everyone is in your way. That the world is conspiring against you, personally, today. That you are uniquely tired and they are uniquely thoughtless.

The chosen story is harder. Maybe the woman screaming at her child has been up since 4am with a parent in the hospital. Maybe the SUV is rushing a kid to the ER. Maybe none of that is true — but you don’t actually know, and the default story isn’t true either. The story is a choice. You may as well choose the one that doesn’t corrode you from the inside.

V

Everyone Worships Something

This is the section that people remember. Wallace argues there is no such thing as not worshipping. The only choice you ever get is what to worship.

Wallace walks through the menu. Money, beauty, status, intellect, power. He’s not saying these things are evil. He’s saying that as objects of worship they all share the same flaw — each one quietly tells you that you don’t have enough of it, and never will. The worship itself is what generates the lack.

The only choice we get is what to worship. David Foster Wallace

The insidious thing about these default worships isn’t that they’re evil. It’s that they’re unconscious. They’re the water. You don’t know you’ve been bowing down to them until they’ve already taken most of your years.

This is water.
This is water.
The discipline of re-noticing — every day
